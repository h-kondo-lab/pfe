\section{From Fermions to the Schr\"odinger Equation}\label{sec:chain}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{One fermionic mode}

A single fermionic mode has occupation number $n \in \{0,1\}$.
\begin{equation}
  Z = 1 + e^\phi, \qquad
  \langle n \rangle = P = \sigma(\phi) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\phi}},
  \qquad \phi \equiv \beta(\mu - \varepsilon).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Canonical free energy}

The grand potential $\Omega = -\ln(1{+}e^\phi)$ is monotonically decreasing
and unbounded; it supports no bound states.
The canonical free energy is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:legendre}
  F = \Omega + \phi\,\langle n \rangle
  = -\ln(1{+}e^\phi) + \phi\,\sigma(\phi)
  \equiv -H(\sigma(\phi)),
\end{equation}
where $H(p) = -p\ln p - (1{-}p)\ln(1{-}p)$ is binary Shannon entropy \cite{shannon}.
This identity is exact (verified to $10^{-16}$).
The canonical ensemble is selected by particle-number conservation.

\subsection{Free energy as effective potential}

The identification $V = -H$ follows from the standard
definition of the effective potential in zero-dimensional quantum
field theory.
For a single fermionic mode with occupation $n \in \{0,1\}$
and source $\phi$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Partition function:
  $Z(\phi) = \sum_{n=0,1} e^{n\phi} = 1 + e^\phi$.
\item Connected generating functional:
  $W(\phi) = \ln Z = \ln(1 + e^\phi)$.
\item Classical field:
  $\sigma = dW/d\phi = e^\phi/(1+e^\phi) = \sigma(\phi)$.
\item Effective action (Legendre transform):
  $\Gamma(\sigma) = \phi\sigma - W(\phi)
  = \sigma\ln\sigma + (1{-}\sigma)\ln(1{-}\sigma) = -H(\sigma)$.
\end{enumerate}
Hence $V(\phi) = \Gamma = -H(\sigma(\phi))$ is the
\emph{exact effective potential} of the 0D fermionic field theory~\cite{grossneveu},
verified numerically to $|\Gamma + H| < 10^{-15}$.

This is not an analogy with Landau--Ginzburg theory.
It is the \emph{definition} of the quantum effective potential
applied to the simplest possible field theory.
The Schr\"odinger equation $-\tfrac{1}{2}\psi'' + \Gamma\,\psi = E\psi$
describes quantum fluctuations of the source field $\phi$ around
the effective potential.
The quantization is not optional: in the classical limit
($\hbar \to 0$, no kinetic term), only the potential minimum
survives ($N = 1$), violating A2---contrast requires multiple
discrete states, which exist only when quantum fluctuations
are present ($N = 3$).
Equivalently, the transfer matrix $T = e^{-H\Delta\tau}$ of the
0D statistical system \cite{aczel} \emph{is} the Schr\"odinger equation in
imaginary time; quantization is not an additional assumption
but the definition of the transfer matrix.
The ``dimension'' traversed by $\tau$ is not a physical
spacetime direction but the field coordinate $\phi$ itself:
the transfer matrix propagates probability amplitudes along the
logit axis, exactly as in lattice QFT with zero spatial dimensions
(the partition function is a path integral over field
configurations, not over positions).
The Gross--Neveu model (Paper~II \S3) provides a concrete
QFT realization of this structure.

\subsection{The natural parameter and the kinetic term}\label{sec:kinetic}

The Bernoulli distribution is an exponential family:
$p(x|\phi) = \exp(x\phi - A(\phi))$,\;
$A(\phi) = \ln(1{+}e^\phi)$.
The coordinate $\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P) = \ln[P/(1{-}P)]$
is the \emph{natural parameter}---the unique coordinate in which
the sufficient statistic $x$ enters linearly.
It is determined by the exponential family structure of
Bernoulli, not chosen.

The kinetic term for fluctuations of $\phi$ is
$T = \tfrac{1}{2}(d\phi/d\tau)^2$:
$\phi$ is the natural parameter of the exponential family,
and in the path integral $\int\!\mathcal{D}\phi\,e^{-S}$
the natural parameter carries flat measure
$\mathcal{D}\phi = \prod_i d\phi_i$.
The canonical action $S = \int[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V]\,d\tau$
gives unit mass.
This is not a choice: the flat measure is fixed by the
exponential-family structure of the Bernoulli distribution.
Moreover, $Z_2 = \sigma(1{-}\sigma) \to 0$ as $\phi \to \pm\infty$
would freeze the field at the domain boundaries, violating
A1 ($P = 1$ must be dynamically reachable, not walled off).

The metrically flat coordinate $\xi = 2\arcsin\sqrt{P} \in [0,\pi]$ gives a compact domain with $N = 1$; the natural parameter $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$ gives a non-compact domain with $N = 3$. The difference is domain topology: $\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P) \to \pm\infty$ as $P \to 0, 1$, making the well wide enough for three half-wavelengths.


\textbf{Exponential family identity.}
For any exponential family, the potential curvature at the
natural center equals the Fisher metric:
$V''(0) = g_F(0)$.
For Bernoulli: $V''(0) = g_F(0) = 1/4$.
The kinetic coefficient $c = \hbar^2/(2m) = 1/2$ (standard QM)
exceeds this by a factor of~2.
The bound-state count $N$ depends on the ratio $c/V''(0)$:
$N = 3$ for $c/V''(0) \in [1.3, 2.5]$.
Standard QM gives $c/V''(0) = 2$, squarely in the $N = 3$ window.

\subsection{The Schr\"odinger equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:schrodinger}
  \boxed{\;
  \left[-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2} - H(\sigma(\phi))\right]\Psi = E\,\Psi
  \;}
\end{equation}
Every element determined: $H$ (Shannon--Khinchin uniqueness \cite{shannon,khinchin}),
$\sigma$ (Fermi--Dirac statistics),
$\phi$ (natural parameter of the exponential family).
The potential $V = -H$ has no free parameters:
every coefficient is fixed by the Bernoulli distribution
($\sigma$, $H$, $\phi$), leaving no room for adjustment.
The kinetic coefficient (unit mass in $\phi$) follows from the
exponential-family flat measure~\cite{cencov,amari} (Section~\ref{sec:kinetic}).

\subsection{Why $\mathrm{PG}(2,3)$: the $N^N = N^3$ theorem}

Projective geometry enters as a consequence of gauge invariance.
Gauge transformations act linearly on $N$ internal states
(standard); the overall phase is unobservable (gauge invariance);
in $d = 3$ spatial dimensions, the gradient $\nabla\phi$ has $d$
components; the independent gauge directions are therefore
the elements of
$\mathbb{F}_N^d \setminus \{0\}$ modulo scalar multiplication
$= \mathrm{PG}(d{-}1, \mathbb{F}_N)$.
With $d = N = 3$: $\mathrm{PG}(2, \mathbb{F}_3)$,
$|\mathrm{PG}| = (N^d{-}1)/(N{-}1) = 13$ points, $52$ flags.
This is not a postulate but a consequence of
linearity $+$ gauge invariance $+$ $d = N = 3$.

\textbf{Why $\mathbb{F}_3$, not $\mathbb{C}^3$.}\;
The Sturm--Liouville theorem guarantees that the
bound-state energies $E_0, E_1, E_2$ are non-degenerate.
A non-degenerate Hamiltonian has a preferred eigenbasis;
its commutant consists of diagonal operators only (Schur).
Gauge symmetry, being exact, must commute with $H$
and therefore preserve the eigenbasis---acting as
\emph{permutations} of the mass eigenstates, not continuous
rotations.
Linear permutations on $N$ labels require a field with $|K| = N$;
since $N = 3$ is prime, $K = \mathbb{F}_3$ is unique.
CKM mixing ($\sin\theta_C = \varepsilon \ll 1$) is a perturbative
off-diagonal correction that does \emph{not} commute with $H$
and is therefore a physical effect, not a gauge symmetry.
The contrast with QCD is instructive: quark colors are
\emph{degenerate} (equal masses), so no preferred basis exists
and the gauge group is continuous ($\mathrm{SU}(3)$ over $\mathbb{C}$);
generations are \emph{non-degenerate}, so the eigenbasis is preferred
and the gauge structure is discrete ($\mathrm{GL}(d, \mathbb{F}_3)$
over $\mathbb{F}_3$).

When $d = N$, the projective structure is \emph{self-referential}.

\begin{theorem}[Self-referential uniqueness]\label{thm:NN}
Let $V = -H(\sigma(\phi))$ with unit mass in $\phi$, and let
$N(d)$ denote the number of bound states of the s-wave equation
in $d$ spatial dimensions,
$-\tfrac{1}{2}u'' + [V(r) + \tfrac{(d{-}1)(d{-}3)}{8r^2}]u = Eu$
(Section~\ref{sec:dimension}).
The following conditions are individually necessary and
jointly satisfied \emph{only} by $N = 3$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] \emph{Fixed point}: $N(d) = d$
  (generation count equals spatial dimension);
\item[(ii)] \emph{Algebraic}: $N^N = N^3$
  (self-referential exponent);
\item[(iii)] \emph{Geometric}: $|\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1, N)| = N^2 + N + 1$
  (projective plane has polynomial size).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
(i)~$N(1) = 3$, $N(2) = 3$, $N(3) = 3$, $N(4) = 2$, $N(d \geq 5) = 0$
(numerical; Theorems~\ref{thm:N3}--\ref{thm:d3}).
The unique solution is $d = 3$.
(Note: $N(d)$ counts bound states of $V = -H$ with a centrifugal
correction; this is distinct from the color-scaled equation
$V = -N_c H$ in Section~\ref{sec:physics}, which has $5$ bound states.)
The physical content of $N(d) = d$: each generation
``fills'' one spatial dimension; excess generations ($N > d$)
destabilize, and excess dimensions ($d > N$) are unoccupied.
(ii)~$N^N = N^3$ iff $N^{N-3} = 1$; for $N \geq 2$ this requires $N = 3$.
(iii)~$|\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1,N)| = (N^N{-}1)/(N{-}1) = N^2{+}N{+}1$
iff $N^N = N^3$, i.e.\ $N = 3$ by~(ii).
\end{proof}

\noindent
The three conditions have independent origins:
(i) from the spectrum of $V = -H$,
(ii) from pure algebra,
(iii) from finite projective geometry.
Their simultaneous satisfaction at $N = 3$ means that the
number of fermion generations, the spatial dimension, and the
gauge structure of the Standard Model are not independent parameters
but consequences of a single self-referential constraint.

\begin{remark}[Why $d = N$]
In most physical theories $d$ and $N$ are independent.
Here the fixed-point equation $N(d) = d$ \emph{forces} $d = N = 3$.
The projective plane $\mathrm{PG}(d{-}1, N) = \mathrm{PG}(N{-}1, N)$
is then self-referential: the field ($\mathbb{F}_N$) and the
ambient dimension ($d$) share the same number.
This self-referential structure exists only for $N = 3$.
\end{remark}

not determined by the theory.
Lovelock's theorem \cite{lovelock} then guarantees that
Einstein's equation $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}$
is the \emph{unique} gravitational dynamics in $d{+}1 = 4$ dimensions
(in $d{+}1 \geq 6$, Gauss--Bonnet terms appear;
the derivation of $d = 3$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:d3}) eliminates them).
Independently, Jacobson \cite{jacobson}: $\delta Q = T\,dS$ on causal
horizons $\to$ Einstein's equation.
If $S = H(P)$ fundamentally, both routes---action-based (Lovelock)
and thermodynamic (Jacobson)---yield Einstein gravity from binary
entropy.

\subsection{Internal--external separation: tensor product structure}\label{sec:tensor}

The decomposition
$\Psi(x,\phi) = \sum_n \psi_n(\phi)\,\chi_n(x)$
is not a dynamical mechanism but a consequence of the tensor product
structure $\mathcal{H}_\mathrm{total} = \mathcal{H}_\mathrm{internal}(\phi) \otimes \mathcal{H}_\mathrm{spacetime}(x)$.
The information coordinate $\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P)$ is not a spatial
direction, just as spin is not a spatial direction.
Three independent arguments support this:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \emph{Generation universality.}
  All gauge couplings are generation-independent, implying zero
  $\phi$--$x$ coupling: $H_\mathrm{int} = H_\mathrm{gauge}(x) \otimes \mathbf{1}_\phi$.

\item \emph{Dimensionless--dimensional separation.}
  The 0D theory computes dimensionless quantities (mass ratios,
  mixing angles, $\sin^2\!\theta_W$). Dimensional quantities
  (absolute masses, $\Lambda_\mathrm{QCD}$) require spacetime input.
  This is exactly what a tensor product predicts: the internal
  factor determines ratios, the spacetime factor supplies units.

\item \emph{5D embedding fails.}
  Promoting $\phi$ to a spatial coordinate
  (domain-wall mechanism \cite{kaplan}) yields an effective potential
  $V_L = H^2 + \phi\sigma(1{-}\sigma) \neq \tfrac{1}{4} + V$,
  which does not reproduce the 0D spectrum.
  $N = 3$ is lost. This failure confirms that $\phi$ is
  categorically distinct from spatial coordinates.
\end{enumerate}

The Schmidt decomposition
$\Psi = \sum_n \psi_n(\phi) \cdot \chi_n(x)$
is always possible in a tensor product and requires no
dynamical justification.
