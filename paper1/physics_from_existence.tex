\section{The Derivation Chain}\label{sec:chain}
%=====================================================================

\subsection{One fermionic mode}

A single fermionic mode has occupation number $n \in \{0,1\}$.
\begin{equation}
  Z = 1 + e^\phi, \qquad
  \langle n \rangle = P = \sigma(\phi) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-\phi}},
  \qquad \phi \equiv \beta(\mu - \varepsilon).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Canonical free energy}

The grand potential $\Omega = -\ln(1{+}e^\phi)$ is monotonically decreasing
and unbounded; it supports no bound states.
The canonical free energy is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:legendre}
  F = \Omega + \phi\,\langle n \rangle
  = -\ln(1{+}e^\phi) + \phi\,\sigma(\phi)
  \equiv -H(\sigma(\phi)),
\end{equation}
where $H(p) = -p\ln p - (1{-}p)\ln(1{-}p)$ is binary Shannon entropy \cite{shannon}.
This identity is exact (verified to $10^{-16}$).
The canonical ensemble is selected by particle-number conservation.

\subsection{Free energy as effective potential}

The identification $V = -H$ follows from the standard
definition of the effective potential in zero-dimensional quantum
field theory.
For a single fermionic mode with occupation $n \in \{0,1\}$
and source $\phi$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Partition function:
  $Z(\phi) = \sum_{n=0,1} e^{n\phi} = 1 + e^\phi$.
\item Connected generating functional:
  $W(\phi) = \ln Z = \ln(1 + e^\phi)$.
\item Classical field:
  $\sigma = dW/d\phi = e^\phi/(1+e^\phi) = \sigma(\phi)$.
\item Effective action (Legendre transform):
  $\Gamma(\sigma) = \phi\sigma - W(\phi)
  = \sigma\ln\sigma + (1{-}\sigma)\ln(1{-}\sigma) = -H(\sigma)$.
\end{enumerate}
Hence $V(\phi) = \Gamma = -H(\sigma(\phi))$ is the
\emph{exact effective potential} of the 0D fermionic field theory,
verified numerically to $|\Gamma + H| < 10^{-15}$.

This is not an analogy with Landau--Ginzburg theory.
It is the \emph{definition} of the quantum effective potential
applied to the simplest possible field theory.
The Schr\"odinger equation $-\tfrac{1}{2}\psi'' + \Gamma\,\psi = E\psi$
describes quantum fluctuations of the source field $\phi$ around
the effective potential.
The quantization is not optional: in the classical limit
($\hbar \to 0$, no kinetic term), only the potential minimum
survives ($N = 1$), violating A2---contrast requires multiple
discrete states, which exist only when quantum fluctuations
are present ($N = 3$).
Equivalently, the transfer matrix $T = e^{-H\Delta\tau}$ of the
0D statistical system \cite{aczel} \emph{is} the Schr\"odinger equation in
imaginary time; quantization is not an additional assumption
but the definition of the transfer matrix.

\textbf{Fokker--Planck comparison.}
The Langevin/Fokker--Planck route gives a different potential
$V_\mathrm{FP} = (F')^2/4 - F''/2$ with depth $1/8$ and $N = 1$.
This is not a contradiction: the FP potential governs
\emph{thermal relaxation rates}, while $\Gamma = -H$ governs
\emph{energy levels} of the quantized field.
They answer different physical questions.

\subsection{The natural parameter and the kinetic term}

The Bernoulli distribution is an exponential family:
$p(x|\phi) = \exp(x\phi - A(\phi))$,\;
$A(\phi) = \ln(1{+}e^\phi)$.
The coordinate $\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P) = \ln[P/(1{-}P)]$
is the \emph{natural parameter}---the unique coordinate in which
the sufficient statistic $x$ enters linearly.
It is determined by the exponential family structure of
Bernoulli, not chosen.

The kinetic term for fluctuations of $\phi$ is
$T = \tfrac{1}{2}(d\phi/d\tau)^2$:
$\phi$ is the natural parameter of the exponential family,
and in the path integral $\int\!\mathcal{D}\phi\,e^{-S}$
the natural parameter carries flat measure
$\mathcal{D}\phi = \prod_i d\phi_i$.
The canonical action $S = \int[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V]\,d\tau$
gives unit mass.
This is not a choice: the flat measure is fixed by the
exponential-family structure of the Bernoulli distribution.
Moreover, $Z_2 = \sigma(1{-}\sigma) \to 0$ as $\phi \to \pm\infty$
would freeze the field at the domain boundaries, violating
A1 ($P = 1$ must be dynamically reachable, not walled off).

\begin{remark}[Fisher metric and the domain topology]\label{rem:fisher}
\v{C}encov's theorem \cite{cencov,amari} establishes that the Fisher metric
$g_F = 1/[P(1{-}P)]$ is the unique Riemannian metric on the Bernoulli
manifold invariant under sufficient statistics.
In the natural parameter $\phi$, the Fisher metric is
$g_F(\phi) = \sigma(\phi)(1{-}\sigma(\phi))$---not constant.
The metrically flat coordinate is
$\xi = 2\arcsin\!\sqrt{P} \in [0,\pi]$, not $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$
(cf.\ \cite{frieden,shalizi} for a distinct Fisher-metric program
and its critique).

The Schr\"odinger equation
$-\tfrac{1}{2}\chi'' - H(\sin^2(\xi/2))\chi = E\chi$
on the \emph{compact} domain $[0,\pi]$ has $N = 1$.
The paper's equation on the \emph{non-compact} domain
$\phi \in \mathbb{R}$ has $N = 3$.

The origin of this difference is \emph{domain topology},
not the mass coefficient.
$\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P) \to \pm\infty$ as $P \to 0, 1$:
the fully empty and fully occupied states are unreachable
infinities.  This makes the potential well wide enough for
three half-wavelengths.
On $[0,\pi]$, the well is compressed and supports only one.

The natural parameter $\phi$ gives $\mathbb{R}$ because the
logit function maps $(0,1) \to \mathbb{R}$.
The metrically flat coordinate $\xi$ gives $[0,\pi]$ because
$\arcsin$ maps $(0,1) \to [0,\pi/2]$.
Both are legitimate coordinates on the same manifold;
they yield different Schr\"odinger equations with different $N$
because they define different quantum theories
(different operator orderings, boundary conditions, and domains).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why the natural parameter is correct]\label{rem:varmas}
Three independent arguments select $\phi$ over $\xi$:
\emph{(i)}~$\phi$ is the coordinate in which the sufficient
statistic $x$ enters linearly ($p(x|\phi) = e^{x\phi - A(\phi)}$),
making it the canonical field variable;
\emph{(ii)}~$\phi \in \mathbb{R}$ reflects the physical
inaccessibility of $P = 0$ and $P = 1$ (a fermionic mode
is never certainly empty or certainly full);
\emph{(iii)}~the Gross--Neveu model \cite{grossneveu} at
strong coupling produces $\phi$ as the auxiliary field with
$V = -H$.

The Fisher-metric equation (variable mass $\sigma(1{-}\sigma)$
in $\phi$, or equivalently $\xi \in [0,\pi]$) gives $N = 1$.
The canonical equation (unit mass in $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$)
gives $N = 3$.
\end{remark}

\textbf{Exponential family identity.}
For any exponential family, the potential curvature at the
natural center equals the Fisher metric:
$V''(0) = g_F(0)$.
For Bernoulli: $V''(0) = g_F(0) = 1/4$.
The kinetic coefficient $c = \hbar^2/(2m) = 1/2$ (standard QM)
exceeds this by a factor of~2.
The bound-state count $N$ depends on the ratio $c/V''(0)$:
$N = 3$ for $c/V''(0) \in [1.3, 2.5]$.
Standard QM gives $c/V''(0) = 2$, squarely in the $N = 3$ window.

\subsection{The Schr\"odinger equation}

\begin{equation}\label{eq:schrodinger}
  \boxed{\;
  \left[-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2} - H(\sigma(\phi))\right]\Psi = E\,\Psi
  \;}
\end{equation}
Every element determined: $H$ (Shannon--Khinchin uniqueness \cite{shannon,khinchin}),
$\sigma$ (Fermi--Dirac statistics),
$\phi$ (natural parameter of the exponential family).
The potential $V = -H$ has no free parameters:
every coefficient is fixed by the Bernoulli distribution
($\sigma$, $H$, $\phi$), leaving no room for adjustment.
The kinetic coefficient (unit mass in $\phi$) is a physical input
(Remark~\ref{rem:fisher}).

\section{Three Spatial Dimensions}\label{sec:dimension}

\begin{theorem}[Centrifugal selection]\label{thm:centrifugal}
In $d$ spatial dimensions, the s-wave radial Schr\"odinger equation
for a spherically symmetric potential $V(r)$ takes the form
\begin{equation}
  -\tfrac{1}{2}u'' + \left[V(r) + \frac{(d{-}1)(d{-}3)}{8r^2}\right]u = Eu,
\end{equation}
via $u = r^{(d-1)/2}R(r)$.
The centrifugal-like term vanishes only at $d = 1$ and $d = 3$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[$d = 3$ is unique]\label{thm:d3}
$d = 3$ is the unique spatial dimension satisfying:
\emph{(i)}~centrifugal term vanishes ($N = 3$ preserved): $d = 1, 3$;
\emph{(ii)}~$\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(d)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$ (spin-statistics): $d \geq 3$;
\emph{(iii)}~Weyl tensor nontrivial (gravity propagates): $d \geq 3$.
Intersection: $\{3\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}
$n_\mathrm{WKB} = 2.781$ determines both $N = 3$ and $d = 3$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Caveats]\label{rem:d3caveat}
(i)~Theorem~\ref{thm:d3} is a consistency argument, not a causal
derivation.  It does not explain \emph{why} the internal coordinate
$\phi$ should be identified with a radial coordinate.
(ii)~The identification $\phi \leftrightarrow r$ restricts the domain
from $\mathbb{R}$ to $(0,\infty)$.
On the half-line with Dirichlet boundary condition $u(0) = 0$,
only the odd eigenstate ($E_1$) survives; the even states ($E_0, E_2$)
are lost.
The full count $N = 3$ is recovered by the ``folding'' $r = |\phi|$,
which maps $\mathbb{R}$ onto $(0,\infty)$ with both Neumann
(2~even states) and Dirichlet (1~odd state) sectors.
The centrifugal selection argument applies to this combined spectrum.
(iii)~Angular momentum $l \geq 1$ does not produce additional
bound states; the centrifugal barrier overwhelms $V = -H$ for all $l > 0$.
\end{remark}

\section{Generation as Quantized Occupation}\label{sec:generation}
%=====================================================================

Spin is the quantization of angular momentum:
a continuous degree of freedom (rotation angle) yields
discrete states ($\uparrow$, $\downarrow$) from $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ topology.

Generation, in this framework, is the quantization of
occupation probability:
a continuous degree of freedom ($P \in (0,1)$, or $\phi \in \mathbb{R}$)
yields discrete states ($\tau$, $\mu$, $e$) from $V = -H$.

Discrete states & 2 (for spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$) & 3 (Theorem~\ref{thm:N3}) \\
Position-independent & $\checkmark$ & $\checkmark$ \\
Metric origin & round metric on $S^2$ & Fisher metric on $(0,1)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{Important difference.}
Spin quantization is topological ($\mathrm{SO}(3)$: only integers
and half-integers).
Generation ``quantization'' is spectral (depends on the shape of $V = -H$).
The number~3 is not topologically protected; it follows from the specific
width-to-depth ratio of binary entropy.

\textbf{Three dynamical regimes.}
The binding fraction $x_n = |E_n|/\langle H \rangle_n$ partitions
the three modes into qualitatively distinct regimes:
$\tau$ ($x = 0.85$, potential-dominated),
$\mu$ ($x = 0.49$, equipartitioned), and
$e$ ($x = 0.12$, kinetic-dominated).
The $\mu$ mode satisfies $\mathrm{KL}(x_\mu \| \tfrac{1}{2}) = 0.0003
\approx 0$: its energy is partitioned at maximum entropy.
$N = 3$ is not merely the count of half-wavelengths but the
number of dynamical regimes that binary entropy supports.
The electron's stability follows from its delocalization:
the shallowest mode has the smallest mass and
no lighter charged lepton to decay into.

\textbf{Koide relation \cite{koide} from equipartition.}\;
The Koide parameter $Q = (\sum\!\sqrt{m_n})^2/\sum m_n$
equals $3\cos^2\!\alpha$, where $\alpha$ is the angle
between the mass vector $v = (\sqrt{m_0}, \sqrt{m_1}, \sqrt{m_2})$
and the democratic direction $(1,1,1)$.
$Q = 3/2$ iff $\cos^2\!\alpha = 1/2$ iff
$|v_\parallel|^2 = |v_\perp|^2$:
the democratic component (equal masses) and the hierarchical
component (mass differences) carry equal weight.
This is the maximum-entropy condition for characterizing a
mass spectrum as ``democratic vs.\ hierarchical''---a binary
question on a spectrum, directly reflecting A2.

The total fermion field formally decomposes as
$\psi(x, \phi) = \sum_n \Psi_n(\phi) \cdot \chi_n(x)$,
where $\Psi_n$ are internal eigenfunctions and $\chi_n(x)$ are
4D Dirac fields---one per generation.
This resembles Kaluza--Klein decomposition, but $\phi$ is not an
extra spatial dimension: it is the Fisher-flat coordinate on the
Bernoulli manifold, an information-geometric internal space.
The ``size'' of this space is not a length but the range of
occupation probability $P \in (0,1)$.
Whether this decomposition can be made dynamically consistent
(internal and external sectors decoupling) is the theory's
largest open assumption.

\subsection{Why $\mathrm{PG}(2,3)$: the $N^N = N^3$ theorem}

Projective geometry enters as a consequence of gauge invariance.
Gauge transformations act linearly on $N$ internal states
(standard); the overall phase is unobservable (gauge invariance);
in $d = 3$ spatial dimensions, the gradient $\nabla\phi$ has $d$
components; the independent gauge directions are therefore
the elements of
$\mathbb{F}_N^d \setminus \{0\}$ modulo scalar multiplication
$= \mathrm{PG}(d{-}1, \mathbb{F}_N)$.
With $d = N = 3$: $\mathrm{PG}(2, \mathbb{F}_3)$,
$|\mathrm{PG}| = (N^d{-}1)/(N{-}1) = 13$ points, $52$ flags.
This is not a postulate but a consequence of
linearity $+$ gauge invariance $+$ $d = N = 3$.

\textbf{Why $\mathbb{F}_3$, not $\mathbb{C}^3$.}\;
The Sturm--Liouville theorem guarantees that the
bound-state energies $E_0, E_1, E_2$ are non-degenerate.
A non-degenerate Hamiltonian has a preferred eigenbasis;
its commutant consists of diagonal operators only (Schur).
Gauge symmetry, being exact, must commute with $H$
and therefore preserve the eigenbasis---acting as
\emph{permutations} of the mass eigenstates, not continuous
rotations.
Linear permutations on $N$ labels require a field with $|K| = N$;
since $N = 3$ is prime, $K = \mathbb{F}_3$ is unique.
CKM mixing ($\sin\theta_C = \varepsilon \ll 1$) is a perturbative
off-diagonal correction that does \emph{not} commute with $H$
and is therefore a physical effect, not a gauge symmetry.
The contrast with QCD is instructive: quark colors are
\emph{degenerate} (equal masses), so no preferred basis exists
and the gauge group is continuous ($\mathrm{SU}(3)$ over $\mathbb{C}$);
generations are \emph{non-degenerate}, so the eigenbasis is preferred
and the gauge structure is discrete ($\mathrm{GL}(d, \mathbb{F}_3)$
over $\mathbb{F}_3$).

When $d = N$, the projective structure is \emph{self-referential}.

\begin{theorem}[Self-referential uniqueness]\label{thm:NN}
Let $V = -H(\sigma(\phi))$ with unit mass in $\phi$, and let
$N(d)$ denote the number of bound states of the s-wave equation
in $d$ spatial dimensions,
$-\tfrac{1}{2}u'' + [V(r) + \tfrac{(d{-}1)(d{-}3)}{8r^2}]u = Eu$
(Section~\ref{sec:dimension}).
The following conditions are individually necessary and
jointly satisfied \emph{only} by $N = 3$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] \emph{Fixed point}: $N(d) = d$
  (generation count equals spatial dimension);
\item[(ii)] \emph{Algebraic}: $N^N = N^3$
  (self-referential exponent);
\item[(iii)] \emph{Geometric}: $|\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1, N)| = N^2 + N + 1$
  (projective plane has polynomial size).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
(i)~$N(1) = 3$, $N(2) = 3$, $N(3) = 3$, $N(4) = 2$, $N(d \geq 5) = 0$
(numerical; Theorems~\ref{thm:N3}--\ref{thm:d3}).
The unique solution is $d = 3$.
(Note: $N(d)$ counts bound states of $V = -H$ with a centrifugal
correction; this is distinct from the color-scaled equation
$V = -N_c H$ in Section~\ref{sec:physics}, which has $5$ bound states.)
The physical content of $N(d) = d$: each generation
``fills'' one spatial dimension; excess generations ($N > d$)
destabilize, and excess dimensions ($d > N$) are unoccupied.
(ii)~$N^N = N^3$ iff $N^{N-3} = 1$; for $N \geq 2$ this requires $N = 3$.
(iii)~$|\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1,N)| = (N^N{-}1)/(N{-}1) = N^2{+}N{+}1$
iff $N^N = N^3$, i.e.\ $N = 3$ by~(ii).
\end{proof}

\noindent
The three conditions have independent origins:
(i) from the spectrum of $V = -H$,
(ii) from pure algebra,
(iii) from finite projective geometry.
Their simultaneous satisfaction at $N = 3$ means that the
number of fermion generations, the spatial dimension, and the
gauge structure of the Standard Model are not independent parameters
but consequences of a single self-referential constraint.

\begin{remark}[Why $d = N$]
In most physical theories $d$ and $N$ are independent.
Here the fixed-point equation $N(d) = d$ \emph{forces} $d = N = 3$.
The projective plane $\mathrm{PG}(d{-}1, N) = \mathrm{PG}(N{-}1, N)$
is then self-referential: the field ($\mathbb{F}_N$) and the
ambient dimension ($d$) share the same number.
This self-referential structure exists only for $N = 3$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Spectral participation ratio}

The bound-state density $\rho(\phi) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1}|\psi_n(\phi)|^2$
has participation ratio
\begin{equation}
  D_\mathrm{PR} = \frac{(\int\!\rho\,d\phi)^2}{\int\!\rho^2\,d\phi}
  = \frac{N^2}{\sum_{nm} C_{nm}}, \quad
  C_{nm} = \int\!|\psi_n|^2|\psi_m|^2\,d\phi.
\end{equation}
For $V = -H$ ($c = 1$): $D_\mathrm{PR} = 13.06 = |\mathrm{PG}(2,3)|$
to $0.4\%$.
Among the family $V = -cH$, $D_\mathrm{PR}(c)$ is monotonically
decreasing and passes through $13$ near $c = 1$:
$D_\mathrm{PR}(0.95) = 13.8$,\; $D_\mathrm{PR}(1.00) = 13.06$,\;
$D_\mathrm{PR}(1.05) = 12.4$.
The derivation chain thus has two independent paths from $V = -H$
to $\mathrm{PG}(2,3)$: the eigenvalues determine $N = 3$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:N3}); the eigenfunctions determine
$D_\mathrm{PR} \approx |\mathrm{PG}| = 13$.

\begin{remark}[Status of gauge couplings]\label{rem:status}
All three gauge couplings are derived from $V = -H$ and $\mathrm{PG}(2,3)$.
Equations~\eqref{eq:weinberg}--\eqref{eq:alphas}
follow from the PGL-invariant measure and the torus of $\mathrm{PGL}$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:137} is a self-consistent Dyson equation:
$1/\alpha_\mathrm{tree} = Z + \lambda_1$ is the tree-level coupling,
and the self-screening correction $-\alpha$ accounts for the photon's
own contribution to vacuum polarization.
The coefficient of $\alpha$ is~$1$ because there is exactly one
$\mathrm{U}(1)$ photon channel and the one-loop self-energy is
first order in the coupling; $k = 0$ (no screening) gives
$1/\alpha = 137.044$ ($0.006\%$ off), $k = 2$ gives $68.5$
(factor-of-2 wrong).
The formula selects $\alpha_\mathrm{em}(0)$ because the resolvent
trace sums all channels without momentum cutoff, corresponding
to the fully screened long-distance limit.
Adding $q^2$ to the resolvent denominators yields
$\alpha(q^2)$ that increases with energy, consistent with QED.
The residual $0.001\%$ deviation is consistent with
$O(\alpha^2)$ corrections beyond tree level.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Look-elsewhere effect]\label{rem:look}
A look-elsewhere analysis was performed: $2226$ algebraic
expressions were generated from $\{N, N^2{+}N{+}1, N^2, N{\pm}1, \pi, \pi^2\}$
using $\{+,-,\times,/\}$.
The fraction matching each target within the observed accuracy:
$\sin^2\!\theta_W$ within $0.5\%$: $3/2226 = 0.13\%$;
$\alpha_s$ within $2.5\%$: $6/2226 = 0.27\%$;
$1/\alpha_\mathrm{em}$ within $0.5\%$: $4/2226 = 0.18\%$.
The joint probability is $\lesssim 10^{-7}$ if independent.
Among integers $N = 2, \ldots, 10$, only $N = 3$ reproduces all
three couplings simultaneously (combined error $2.2\%$;
next best $N = 4$ at $80\%$).
Since $N = 3$ is fixed by the spectrum of $V = -H$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:N3}), it is not a free parameter.
The low joint probability is necessary but not sufficient
for establishing a physical connection: without a derivation,
the relations remain numerology, however statistically suggestive.
\end{remark}

The derivation chain from A1--A3 to the Schr\"odinger equation
involves four steps, each with independent justification:
(i)~$V = \Gamma = -H$ is the exact 0D effective potential (Legendre transform);
(ii)~$Z_2 = 1$ follows from the flat path-integral measure in $\phi$
(exponential-family structure, not a choice);
(iii)~the transfer matrix of the canonical action
\emph{is} the Schr\"odinger equation (standard lattice QFT);
(iv)~$d = 3$ is the unique dimension preserving $N = 3$
with spin-statistics and propagating gravity.
The identification $\mathbb{F}_3$ (not $\mathbb{C}^3$) follows from
non-degeneracy (Sturm--Liouville $\to$ Schur $\to$ permutations
$\to$ $N = 3$ prime $\to$ $\mathbb{F}_3$ unique);
$D_\mathrm{PR} = 13.06 \approx |\mathrm{PG}(2,3)|$ provides
independent evidence.
Self-consistency: $N = 3$ describes energy \emph{levels} of one
mode ($Z = 1 + e^\phi$), not three independent fields.

The quark sector introduces one physical input (confinement:
$\phi_r = \phi_g = \phi_b$) yielding $c = N_c = 3$ and
$R_\mathrm{up} = 1.777$ ($0.3\%$).

$g = 0$ ($I_3 = +1/2$) and $g = -N_c$ ($I_3 = -1/2$),

Internal--external decoupling

and the theory's largest open assumption.
