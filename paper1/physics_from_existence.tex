\section{Introduction}
%=====================================================================

The Standard Model is the most successful theory of particle physics.
It describes all known elementary particle interactions and has withstood
extensive experimental verification, including the discovery of the Higgs boson~\cite{atlas,cms}.
Yet the theory contains
26 free parameters\footnote{The minimal Standard Model with massless
neutrinos has 19 free parameters~\cite{pdg}; adding three neutrino
masses and four PMNS parameters gives 26.  We write ``26'' throughout;
some authors count 25 by absorbing $v_\mathrm{EW}$ into the
overall energy scale.}---fermion masses (Yukawa couplings),
mixing angles, gauge couplings, the Higgs self-coupling, and CP phases.
These values must be measured experimentally and inserted by hand;
why they take the values they do is not explained within the Standard Model.
The generation count $N = 3$ is likewise an unexplained input.
Despite many attempts---grand unified theories (GUTs), supersymmetry (SUSY),
string theory---no theory has simultaneously determined all 26 parameters
with zero free parameters~\cite{xing}.

This paper addresses the problem starting from a single equation.
The point of departure is one of the most fundamental facts in physics:
\textbf{the occupation number of each fermionic mode is 0 or~1.}
Three axioms formalize this:
Existence ($P = 1$), Contrast ($P \neq 1$ required for information),
and Observation (detection probability $= P$).
The partition function of this two-valued degree of freedom $n \in \{0,1\}$
is $Z = 1 + e^\phi$, and its canonical free energy is, by Legendre transform,
$V = -H(\sigma(\phi))$, where $H$ is the binary Shannon entropy,
$\sigma(\phi)$ is the Fermi--Dirac distribution (sigmoid function),
and $\phi = \operatorname{logit}(P)$ is the natural parameter of the
occupation probability~\cite{aczel}.
This equality is an algebraic identity,
involving no approximation and no adjustable parameter.
$V = -H$ is not imported from outside the Standard Model;
it is a structure the SM already contains the moment it includes fermions.

The Schr\"odinger equation with $V = -H$ as potential has exactly three
bound states ($N = 3$ fermion generations).
The spatial dimension $d = 3$ (why we live in three-dimensional space)
follows from spin-statistics, gravity propagation, and bound-state
preservation;
the gauge structure $\mathrm{PG}(2,3)$
(from which the Standard Model gauge group emerges)
from the self-referential coincidence $N = d = 3$
($N^N = N^d$ holds for $N \geq 2$ only when $N = 3$).
These determine the structure of the Standard Model Lagrangian
and, through it, over 20 of its parameters---including
$1/\alpha_\mathrm{em} = 137.0367$ ($0.0006\%$),
$\sin^2\!\theta_W = 3/13$ ($0.2\%$),
the charged-lepton mass ratio $R = 1.5293$ ($0.006\%$),
Koide's $Q \approx 3/2$ ($\delta = -1.5 \times 10^{-5}$),

\section{From Fermions to the Schr\"odinger Equation}\label{sec:chain}
%=====================================================================

As stated in \S1, $V = -H(\sigma(\phi))$ follows algebraically from the
fermionic occupation number $n \in \{0,1\}$.
This section makes the derivation explicit and establishes the
Schr\"odinger equation with $V = -H$ as potential.

The physical fact that fermionic occupation is 0 or~1 can be formulated
as a consequence of three more fundamental principles:

\noindent\textbf{A1~(Existence).}\;
An entity with occupation probability $P = 1$ exists.
This is the existence of a fermionic mode itself,
defining a two-state system $n \in \{0,1\}$.
Algebraically, $n^2 = n$ (idempotency)---the expression of the
Pauli exclusion principle~\cite{pauli}.

\noindent\textbf{A2~(Contrast).}\;
$P = 1$ is meaningful only if $P \neq 1$ is also possible.
Existence alone carries no information;
the contrast between ``occupied'' and ``unoccupied'' is required.
This demands that the occupation probability $\sigma \in (0,1)$ takes
non-trivial values.
A dynamical consequence: $\sigma \in (0,1)$ requires vacuum stability,
and Ostrogradsky's theorem~\cite{ostrogradsky} then excludes higher-order equations
of motion, restricting operators to $\dim \leq 4$
(renormalizability).

\noindent\textbf{A3~(Observation).}\;
The detection probability equals~$P$.
This is the content of the Born rule, uniquely determined within
quantum theory by Gleason's theorem~\cite{gleason}
(the single-mode Hilbert space embeds in the full
Fock space, where $\dim \geq 3$).

\noindent
The three axioms articulate the most primitive premises of physics:
the existence of fermions~(A1), the necessity of contrast~(A2),
and the procedure of observation~(A3).
From these, the Bernoulli distribution is determined,
and the derivation below proceeds.
Note that one may alternatively take Fermi--Dirac statistics as an
experimental fact and bypass the axiomatic formulation---both paths
arrive at the same $V = -H$
(detailed in Paper~II~\cite{paper2}, \S2).

\subsection{The effective potential $V = -H$}

Consider a single quantum state of a fermion
(momentum~$\mathbf{p}$ and spin~$s$ fixed),
with occupation number $n \in \{0,1\}$ and
source $\phi = \beta(\mu - \varepsilon)$.
The grand potential $\Omega = -\ln(1{+}e^\phi)$ is monotonically
decreasing and unbounded below; it supports no bound states.
The Legendre transform to the canonical ensemble produces
a bounded potential.
The partition function is $Z(\phi) = 1 + e^\phi$,
and its logarithm $W = \ln Z = \ln(1 + e^\phi)$
generates the classical field
$\sigma = dW/d\phi = 1/(1+e^{-\phi})$,
the Fermi--Dirac distribution.
The Legendre transform yields the effective potential:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:legendre}
  V(\phi) = \phi\sigma - W(\phi)
  = \sigma\ln\sigma + (1{-}\sigma)\ln(1{-}\sigma) = -H(\sigma),
\end{equation}
where $H(p) = -p\ln p - (1{-}p)\ln(1{-}p)$
is binary Shannon entropy~\cite{shannon}.
\noindent
$V = -H$ is an algebraic identity: the canonical free energy of
a single fermionic mode \emph{is} the negative of its
Shannon entropy.
The same result arises as the exact effective action of the
Gross--Neveu model~\cite{grossneveu} with $N_f = 1$
(Paper~II~\cite{paper2}, \S3).

\subsection{The natural parameter and the kinetic term}\label{sec:kinetic}

The Bernoulli distribution is an exponential family,
and $\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P)$ is its natural parameter.
The natural parameter carries flat measure in the path integral,
giving the canonical action
$S = \int[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V]\,d\tau$ with unit mass.
This choice is uniquely determined by the exponential-family structure:
the Fisher metric~\cite{fisher} of the Bernoulli family
is $g_F(\phi) = \sigma(1{-}\sigma)$;
in natural coordinates the $e$-connection is flat,
giving unit coefficient for the kinetic term~\cite{cencov,amari,ay}.
Any other mass would introduce a free parameter not present
in the Bernoulli distribution
(proof in Paper~II~\cite{paper2}, \S2).

\subsection{The Schr\"odinger equation}

Quantizing the fluctuations of $\phi$ around the
effective potential $V = -H$ (\S2.1) with the
unit-mass kinetic term (\S2.2) gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schrodinger}
  \boxed{\;
  \left[-\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2} - H(\sigma(\phi))\right]\Psi = E\,\Psi
  \;}
\end{equation}
\noindent
This is the Schr\"odinger equation with $V = -H$ as potential.

\section{Three Bound States}\label{sec:theorem}
%=====================================================================

Why are there exactly three generations of fermions ($\tau$, $\mu$, $e$)?
The potential $V = -H$ is a well:
deep at the center ($V = -\ln 2$ at $\phi = 0$)
and rising to zero at $\phi \to \pm\infty$.
In quantum mechanics, a well confines discrete energy levels---just as
a hydrogen atom confines electron orbits.
A deep, wide well accommodates many levels;
a shallow, narrow well accommodates few.
$V = -H$ is shallow (depth $\ln 2 \approx 0.693$)
and its shape is entirely fixed by Shannon entropy.
The number of confined levels is the number of fermion generations.

Solving the equation from \S2 determines this number.
First, we estimate how many standing waves fit in the well.
The semiclassical (WKB) count gives
\begin{equation}
  n_\mathrm{WKB} = \frac{1}{\pi}\!\int\!\sqrt{2H}\,d\phi = 2.781\,;
\end{equation}
the third state fits; the fourth does not.

The result $N = 3$ depends on the coordinate.
The metrically flat coordinate
$\xi = 2\arcsin\!\sqrt{P} \in [0,\pi]$ gives a compact domain
with only $N = 1$; the natural parameter
$\phi = \mathrm{logit}(P) \in \mathbb{R}$ gives a non-compact domain
with $N = 3$.
The difference is domain topology:
$\phi \to \pm\infty$ as $P \to 0, 1$,
making the well wide enough for three half-wavelengths.
The coordinate $\phi$ is not chosen but determined
by the exponential-family structure (\S2.2).

The bound-state count also depends on the ratio
of kinetic coefficient to potential curvature:
$N = 3$ holds for $c/V''(0) \in [1.3,\, 2.5]$.
With unit mass (\S2.2) and $V''(0) = 1/4$,
$c/V''(0) = 2$---squarely in the $N = 3$ window.

This can be made rigorous.
At least three bound states exist:
three variational trial functions can be constructed,
each with negative energy expectation value (Courant--Fischer).
At most three exist:
the P\"oschl--Teller potential~\cite{poschl}
$V_\mathrm{PT} = -(\ln 2)/\cosh^2(\alpha\phi)$
with $\alpha = 0.40$ satisfies
$V_\mathrm{PT}(\phi) \leq V(\phi)$ for all~$\phi$
(same depth, wider tails; verified numerically).
Its analytic spectrum ($s = 2.49$) supports exactly three
bound states.
By the Sturm comparison theorem,
$V = -H$---being shallower or equal everywhere---supports
no more.
Therefore $N = 3$ exactly, with eigenvalues
\begin{equation}
  E_0 = -0.4850,\quad E_1 = -0.1591,\quad E_2 = -0.01042.
\end{equation}

\noindent
The third bound state ($\psi_2$, the electron) has binding energy
$|E_2| = 0.010$---only $1.5\%$ of the well depth.
It is marginally bound.
The WKB deficit
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon = 3 - n_\mathrm{WKB} = 0.219
\end{equation}
measures this marginality and determines the Cabibbo angle
and all CKM/PMNS mixing parameters
(\S\ref{sec:gauge}--\ref{sec:physics}).

The result $N = 3$ depends on the choice of entropy.
Replacing Shannon entropy ($q = 1$) with R\'enyi entropy ($q \neq 1$)
changes the well depth: $q < 1$ deepens it ($N = 4$);
$q > 1$ makes it shallower ($N = 2$).
Shannon is the unique additive entropy
(Khinchin's theorem~\cite{khinchin}).
$N = 3$ is the spectral consequence of entropy being additive.

\begin{theorem}[$N = 3$]\label{thm:N3}
The Schr\"odinger equation~\eqref{eq:schrodinger} with $V = -H(\sigma(\phi))$
has exactly three bound states:
$E_0 = -0.4850$, $E_1 = -0.1591$, $E_2 = -0.01042$.
The fourth eigenvalue $E_3 > 0$ lies in the continuum.
A fourth generation of fermions is forbidden.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}\;
Variational (Courant--Fischer) for $N \geq 3$;
Sturm comparison with P\"oschl--Teller~\cite{poschl}
for $N \leq 3$.\hfill$\square$

\section{Three Spatial Dimensions}\label{sec:dimension}
%=====================================================================

Next, we solve the Schr\"odinger equation of \S2 in $d$ spatial
dimensions.
When a spherically symmetric potential is placed in $d$-dimensional
space, the angular degrees of freedom can be separated out,
leaving a radial equation.
This radial equation is not the same as the original
one-dimensional equation: it acquires an extra term
$(d{-}1)(d{-}3)/(8r^2)$ that acts as an additional effective force.
The strength and sign of this force depend on $d$:

\begin{center}\small
\begin{tabular}{ccc}
\toprule
$d$ & $(d{-}1)(d{-}3)/8$ & Effect on $N$ \\
\midrule
1 & 0 & no change \\
2 & $-1/8$ & attractive $\to$ extra states appear \\
3 & \textbf{0} & \textbf{no change: $N = 3$ preserved} \\
4 & $+3/8$ & repulsive $\to$ electron lost \\
$\geq 5$ & $> 1$ & strongly repulsive \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
At $d = 1$ and $d = 3$ the extra term vanishes
and the three bound states of \S\ref{sec:theorem} survive intact.
At $d \geq 4$ the term is repulsive and pushes out the
marginally bound electron ($|E_2|$ is only $1.5\%$ of the well depth).
The three generations can exist only in $d = 1$ or $d = 3$.

Two further physical requirements eliminate $d = 1$.
First, fermions require half-integer spin obeying the
spin-statistics theorem.
The spin-statistics connection requires $d \geq 3$
spatial dimensions: $\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(d)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$
for $d \geq 3$, giving a unique double cover
$\mathrm{Spin}(d)$ with finite-dimensional spinor
representations.\footnote{$\mathrm{SO}(2)$ has
$\pi_1 = \mathbb{Z}$ and admits a double cover,
but the resulting representations are
one-dimensional and do not support the
spin-statistics connection needed for fermionic fields.}
Second, gravity must propagate as a dynamical field.
In $d \leq 2$ the gravitational field has no local degrees
of freedom (the Weyl tensor vanishes identically).
Propagating gravity requires $d \geq 3$.

The intersection of all three conditions---bound-state preservation,
spin-statistics, and gravity---is $d = 3$ alone.
Three-dimensional space is not an input; it is therefore a consequence.

\begin{theorem}[$d = 3$]\label{thm:d3}
$d = 3$ is the unique spatial dimension satisfying:
\emph{(i)}~bound-state preservation ($N = 3$ unchanged): $d = 1, 3$;
\emph{(ii)}~spin-statistics connection holds: $d \geq 3$;
\emph{(iii)}~gravity propagates (Weyl tensor nontrivial): $d \geq 3$.
Intersection: $\{3\}$.
\end{theorem}

\noindent\textit{Proof.}\;
(i)~centrifugal term $(d{-}1)(d{-}3)/(8r^2) = 0$ iff $d = 1$ or $3$;
(ii)~$\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(d)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$ for $d \geq 3$;
(iii)~Weyl tensor has $d^2(d^2{-}1)/12 - d(d{+}1)/2$ independent
components, positive only for $d \geq 3$.\hfill$\square$

\subsection{Origin of the gauge structure}

What gauge structure follows from $N = d = 3$?
Gauge transformations act linearly on the $N$ internal states
and leave the overall phase unobservable.
The key question is: what field do these transformations act over?

The bound-state energies $E_0, E_1, E_2$ are non-degenerate
(Sturm--Liouville).
This is the crucial difference from color:
quark colors are degenerate, so no preferred basis exists
and the color gauge group is continuous
($\mathrm{SU}(3)$ over $\mathbb{C}$).
Generations are non-degenerate, so the generation space
has a preferred eigenbasis and any symmetry that preserves
this basis can only \emph{permute} the eigenstates.
Permutation matrices acting on $N = 3$ labels require a field
of order~$3$; since $3$ is prime, $\mathbb{F}_3$ is unique.

With the field $\mathbb{F}_3$ established,
the gauge directions in $d = 3$ spatial dimensions
are the nonzero vectors in $\mathbb{F}_3^3$
modulo scalar multiplication.
This is the projective plane $\mathrm{PG}(2, \mathbb{F}_3)$:
a finite geometry with 13~points and 52~flags.

The coincidence $d = N$ makes the projective structure
\emph{self-referential}: the field ($\mathbb{F}_N$) and the
ambient dimension ($d$) share the same number.
This can happen only for $N = 3$.
The equation $N^N = N^d$ holds for $N \geq 2$ only when $N = 3$
($N^{N-3} = 1$ requires $N = 3$),
and the projective plane has polynomial size only because of this:
\begin{equation}
  |\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1,N)| = \frac{N^N{-}1}{N{-}1}
  = N^2{+}N{+}1 = 13.
\end{equation}
The number of fermion generations, the spatial dimension,
and the gauge geometry are not independent:
they are locked together by a single self-referential constraint.

\begin{theorem}[Self-referential uniqueness]\label{thm:NN}
$N = 3$ is the unique integer $N \geq 2$ satisfying simultaneously:
\emph{(i)}~$N(d) = d$ (generation count equals spatial dimension);
\emph{(ii)}~$N^N = N^3$ (self-referential exponent);
\emph{(iii)}~$|\mathrm{PG}(N{-}1, N)| = N^2 + N + 1$
(projective plane has polynomial size).
\end{theorem}
\noindent\textit{Proof.}\;
(i)~$N(d) = d$ has unique solution $d = 3$
(\S\ref{sec:theorem}--\S\ref{sec:dimension});
(ii)~$N^{N-3} = 1$ for $N \geq 2$ requires $N = 3$;
(iii)~follows from~(ii).\hfill$\square$

  \underbrace{N^2}_{9\;\text{color}} +

The 9~points off the line correspond to the color sector,
the 3~points on $L$ (other than $P_0$) to the weak sector,

Before this choice, all 13 directions are equivalent;
the choice of $(P_0, L)$ is the geometric expression of

The gauge group $\mathrm{SU}(3) \times \mathrm{SU}(2) \times

\subsection{Gauge couplings}

This geometry determines all three gauge coupling constants.

  the $\overline{\mathrm{MS}}$ value $\sin^2\!\hat\theta_W(M_Z) = 0.23122 \pm 0.00003$~\cite{pdg}.
  The $0.2\%$ deviation is consistent in sign and magnitude

$m_W/m_Z = \cos\theta_W = \sqrt{10/13} = 0.8771$

\subsection{Why $\phi$ and $x$ are independent}\label{sec:tensor}
